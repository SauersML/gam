TO: A mathematician with no limits on depth, patience, or invention.
RE: Find the optimal spline. Or whatever should replace the spline.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
THE PROBLEM
----------------------------------------------------------------------

We observe n points (x_i, y_i), x_i in R^d (d from 1 to a few dozen,
n from thousands to hundreds of millions), y_i noisy. We want to
estimate a flexible function f, with honest uncertainty, as one term
inside a larger model: other terms alongside it, non-Gaussian
likelihoods, identifiability constraints, and hyperparameters chosen
by marginal likelihood — which means the surrounding machinery needs
derivatives of the whole fitted object with respect to everything.

The estimator must run on physical machines: finite memory, deep
memory hierarchies, wide parallelism, arithmetic that is fast for
some operations and slow for others, floating point that rounds.

Today this is done with splines and kernel methods: choose a
smoothness functional, derive its minimizer, evaluate the resulting
basis pointwise, pay whatever linear algebra falls out. Every named
construction in the literature fixed the mathematics first and
accepted the computation that resulted. The reverse direction — and
every direction orthogonal to both — is essentially unexplored. We
do not believe the optimum has been found. We are not convinced the
right *question* has been posed. That is your opening.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
THE NEEDS — separated from the habits
----------------------------------------------------------------------

We have tried to distinguish what we actually require from what we
merely inherited. Hold us to this distinction; if you find an item
below is itself a habit in disguise, demote it and say why.

NEEDS:

  N1. RESIST NOISE, DON'T CHASE POINTS. The data is noisy; the fit
      must not interpolate individual observations. But the family
      must be rich enough to reach interpolation in its unpenalized
      limit — richness with a governor, not a small model.

  N2. BRIDGE GAPS, DON'T SAG. Where data thins out between regions
      that have data, the fit must follow continuity from the flanks
      — pass smoothly between its neighbors — not revert toward a
      global mean. (If A and C are high and sparse B lies between
      them, the estimate at B is high, even when most of the data
      lives elsewhere at low values.) Mean reversion in gaps is a
      bias imposed by stationarity assumptions, not something the
      data asked for. The same applies to trends: genuine
      large-scale structure must not be shrunk away. N1 and N2
      together: shrink against noise pointwise, never toward the
      mean across gaps.

  N3. AS SMOOTH AS THE DATA ALLOWS. The prior favors smoothness; the
      data chooses the level, through a continuous dial that is
      differentiable (the surrounding model optimizes over it by
      marginal likelihood) with a well-defined effective degrees of
      freedom. This dial is the most load-bearing property in
      practice. Spatially varying smoothness is optional — worth
      having only if it costs nearly nothing.

  N4. LEARNED GEOMETRY. Invariance to meaningless transformations
      (units, per-axis affine maps) is the floor. Beyond it, the
      metric in which "near" and "smooth" are measured must itself
      be learnable — per-axis scales at minimum. Isotropy in the
      learned metric is a sensible default symmetry, not dogma.

  N5. UQ-COMPATIBLE. The construction must not obstruct honest
      uncertainty: valid uncertainty for any linear functional of
      the fit (values, derivatives, integrals, contrasts) must be
      obtainable downstream at reasonable cost, accounting for any
      data-dependent choices the method makes along the way
      (discrete selection silently poisons naive intervals). Where
      data is absent, uncertainty grows — never silently confident
      off-support.

  N6. COMPOSABLE. The term must live inside penalized likelihoods
      with other terms; admit non-Gaussian likelihoods; respect
      identifiability constraints; expose derivatives with respect
      to inputs, coefficients, and hyperparameters. (For
      calibration: linearity in coefficients is what currently buys
      exact Gaussian inference, closed-form uncertainty, and
      tractable hyperparameter derivatives at once. Abandon it
      knowingly, replacing what it buys.)

  N7. KNOWN ERROR. Every output must come with known accuracy:
      either an a-priori theorem covering all inputs the method
      accepts, or an a-posteriori certificate computed alongside the
      answer, with a correct fallback when the certificate fails.
      Which mechanism is yours to choose; what is disqualifying is
      unknown error. (In our experience, accuracy often hinges on
      data-dependent quantities — ranges, conditioning, geometry —
      that no a-priori bound sees. Methods that are honest about
      this tend to need the certificate. Methods exact by
      construction need nothing.)

HABITS (inherited, discardable without argument):

  Radial bases. Kernels. Reproducing-kernel Hilbert spaces. Basis
  expansions of fixed size. Variational formulations. Stationarity.
  Gaussianity of the implied prior. The word "spline."

----------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT "OPTIMAL" MUST ANSWER TO
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Judge any candidate against all of these at once, as one object:

  1. STATISTICAL: risk under honest assumption classes; the needs
     N1–N5 above; no hidden bias against structure the user cares
     about.

  2. COMPUTATIONAL: total cost to fit, evaluate, differentiate, and
     (where applicable) certify — as functions of n, d, and
     accuracy. Constants matter. Memory traffic matters. Parallel
     depth matters. Worst case and typical case both matter.

  3. ACCURACY-KNOWN: N7, by whichever mechanism.

  4. COMPOSABLE: N6, in full.

If these pull against each other, demonstrate it: exhibit the
frontier and the trade-off, with proof where the claim is
mathematical. If they do not, exhibit the object that achieves them
simultaneously. Either result is an answer. Asserted tension without
evidence is not.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
STANDARDS OF EVIDENCE
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Match the claim to its kind:

  - Mathematical claims (risk bounds, invariances, error bounds,
    lower bounds, obstructions): proof.
  - Machine-performance claims (constants, memory behavior, parallel
    scaling, wall-clock): asymptotic analysis PLUS reproducible
    measurement on stated hardware. We know proofs of constants are
    not generally feasible; we will not pretend otherwise, and
    neither should you. Theory that predicts and measurement that
    confirms, together, are the standard.
  - Frontier claims you cannot yet settle: state them as
    conjectures, flagged as such, with the falsifiable prediction
    that would settle them.

A claim dressed in the wrong evidence — a "proof" of wall-clock, a
benchmark standing in for a theorem — is worse than an honest gap.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
FREEDOMS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

You are not required to produce anything resembling current methods.
The habits list above is explicitly discardable. Beyond that: you may
redefine the estimand, the noise model, the notion of smoothness, the
notion of evaluation, or the boundary between "model" and "algorithm"
— provided you state what you changed and show the replacement still
serves needs N1–N7. If the best object is adaptive, randomized,
implicit, defined only through an algorithm, or stranger than that:
fine. The needs are the only contract.

Do not assume current hardware is permanent; do assume physics —
locality, parallelism, finite precision — is.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT AN ANSWER LOOKS LIKE
----------------------------------------------------------------------

  - The construction, stated precisely.
  - Its statistical properties, with assumption classes explicit,
    evidenced to the standards above.
  - In what sense it is optimal, and against what class of
    alternatives: matching lower bounds where you can get them,
    obstruction results or flagged conjectures where you cannot.
    "Best I found" is a progress report, not an answer.
  - The algorithm, with cost and accuracy analysis, to the level a
    competent engineer could implement without further invention.
  - The failure modes, stated plainly: where it loses to existing
    methods, and by how much.

If you discover instead that the question is malposed — that
"optimal spline" dissolves under the right formalization — then say
exactly what dissolves it and what the well-posed successor question
is. That, too, is an answer worth having.

Take whatever path through mathematics you need. We have given you
the needs we actually have, the habits we know we inherited, and
nothing else, on purpose.
